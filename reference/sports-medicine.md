# Sports medicine reference

Safety-first reasoning for training around injury, illness, labs, and recovery. **This is decision support, not diagnosis or treatment.** When in doubt, refer to a licensed clinician. Err conservative.

## Red flags — STOP training the area and seek medical care

**Emergency (call emergency services):**
- Chest pain/pressure/tightness, esp. with exertion; pain radiating to arm/jaw; sweating, nausea with it.
- Severe shortness of breath, fainting/syncope, or new palpitations with dizziness.
- Sudden severe headache, slurred speech, facial droop, one-sided weakness/numbness, vision loss (stroke signs).
- Loss of bladder/bowel control with back pain + leg numbness (cauda equina).
- Acute injury with gross deformity, joint locked, or inability to bear weight at all.

**Urgent (see a clinician promptly, don't train through it):**
- Exertional chest discomfort, unusual breathlessness, or exercise intolerance that's new.
- Sharp/radiating limb pain, progressive numbness/tingling/weakness.
- Joint that gives way, locks, or had a "pop" + rapid swelling.
- Pain worse at night, at rest, or progressively worsening despite rest.
- Fever + hot swollen joint; unexplained weight loss; persistent unexplained fatigue.

## Pain rules during/after training

- **Traffic-light for known/managed issues (rehab loading):** pain ≤3/10 that stays stable and settles within 24h is usually acceptable; 4–5/10 → modify (reduce load/ROM); >5/10, sharp, or lingering >24h → stop that exercise.
- Sharp, pinpoint, or radiating pain = stop. Dull, diffuse muscular fatigue = usually fine.
- Pain that changes your movement pattern (limping, compensating) = stop; you're training the compensation.
- Swelling, heat, or instability after a session = back off and reassess.

## Working around common injuries (general load-management principles)

> Don't fully rest unless told to by a clinician — relative rest + load modification usually beats total rest. Find pain-free variations, keep training everything else.

- **Low back (non-red-flag):** keep moving; hip-hinge pattern coached; reduce spinal load/ROM that flares it; build trunk endurance (McGill big-3: curl-up, side plank, bird-dog), then reload gradually. Avoid end-range loaded flexion early.
- **Knee (patellofemoral/tendinopathy):** isometrics for pain relief (e.g. wall sit/Spanish squat), then slow heavy resistance through tolerable ROM; manage volume spikes; strengthen quads/glutes/calves.
- **Shoulder (impingement/rotator cuff):** avoid painful overhead/behind-neck early; build scapular control + cuff (external rotation, scaption), progress press ROM as tolerated.
- **Achilles/patellar tendinopathy:** tendons love progressive heavy slow loading; isometrics for in-season pain; avoid sudden volume/intensity spikes; modify, don't rest fully.
- **Ankle sprain:** early controlled loading, balance/proprioception (single-leg work), restore dorsiflexion; return to impact when single-leg hop is pain-free and symmetric.
- **General tendinopathy principle:** symptoms lag load — judge by 24h response, progress slowly, isometrics calm pain, heavy-slow builds capacity.

## Return-to-training criteria (graded)

Progress to the next stage only when the current one is pain-free and symmetric:
1. Pain-free daily function & full (or near-full) ROM.
2. Bodyweight/low-load movement pain-free.
3. Progressive loading to ≥~90% of the uninjured side's strength.
4. Sport/movement-specific loading (speed, change of direction, plyometrics).
5. Full return with monitoring of 24h response.

## Cardiovascular screening before hard training

- Increase intensity gradually if previously sedentary, older, or with risk factors (hypertension, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, family history of early cardiac disease/sudden death, BMI high).
- Any cardiac symptoms (above) → medical clearance before vigorous/HIIT work.
- **Blood pressure:** if known uncontrolled/high (e.g. ≥180/110), avoid heavy straining/Valsalva and high-intensity work until controlled; favor moderate aerobic + clinician guidance.

## Lab markers relevant to training (context, not diagnosis)

> Interpret trends, not single values; reference ranges vary by lab and individual. Route abnormal/uncertain values to a physician.

- **Ferritin / iron studies / hemoglobin:** low → impaired endurance, fatigue; common in endurance and menstruating athletes. Don't supplement iron without testing.
- **Vitamin D (25-OH):** low → bone/muscle/immune effects; supplement to sufficiency.
- **Lipids (LDL, HDL, triglycerides):** guide conditioning emphasis & diet; aerobic exercise + diet improve profile.
- **Glucose / HbA1c:** insulin sensitivity; resistance + aerobic training help; coordinate with clinician if diabetic.
- **TSH/thyroid:** affects metabolism, recovery, energy.
- **Testosterone / cortisol / LH-FSH:** chronically suppressed sex hormones or elevated cortisol can signal overtraining or low energy availability (RED-S).
- **CK (creatine kinase):** very high after unaccustomed/eccentric work; extreme + dark urine → rhabdomyolysis risk (medical emergency).
- **hs-CRP:** systemic inflammation marker.
- **Electrolytes (Na/K/Mg), kidney (creatinine/eGFR), liver enzymes:** context for hydration, cramps, and supplement/training safety.

## Recovery & readiness

- **Sleep** is the #1 recovery and performance lever — target 7–9h; chronic deficit blunts gains, raises injury/illness risk, impairs glucose/appetite regulation.
- **HRV / resting HR trends:** falling HRV / rising resting HR over days = accumulating fatigue or illness → ease off.
- **Readiness check inputs:** sleep quality, soreness, mood/motivation, resting HR, life stress, appetite, joint achiness. Use to autoregulate today's dose.
- **Recovery tools that work:** sleep, nutrition (protein + total calories + carbs), easy movement/active recovery, stress management. Nice-to-haves: massage, mobility, sauna, cold (note: cold immediately post-resistance may blunt hypertrophy — separate from key lifting).

## Overtraining & RED-S warning signs

- Performance decline despite training, persistent fatigue, elevated resting HR/low HRV, mood disturbance, poor sleep, frequent illness/injury, loss of libido, menstrual disturbance, unexplained weight change.
- Response: pull back volume/intensity (deload or rest), restore energy availability and sleep, reassess. Persistent → medical evaluation.

## Illness & training ("neck check" heuristic)

- Symptoms above the neck (mild runny nose, sore throat), no fever → light/moderate training usually OK, reduce intensity.
- Symptoms below the neck (chest congestion, body aches, fever, GI illness) → rest until recovered. **Never do intense exercise with a fever or active systemic illness** (myocarditis risk).

## Environmental

- **Heat:** acclimatize over 1–2 weeks; reduce intensity, hydrate + sodium; watch for heat illness (confusion, stops sweating, very high temp = emergency).
- **Altitude:** reduce intensity initially, hydrate, expect lower performance until acclimatized.
